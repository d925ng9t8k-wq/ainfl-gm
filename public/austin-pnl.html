2 How the Model Works

Both Lower and Rapid are IMBs. Same lender fees to the borrower. Same 300 bps blended branch margin across all products. The revenue line is identical.

In both structures, Austin keeps all branch margin revenue after paying staff payroll and branch expenses. He runs the P&L.

The variance between Lower and Rapid comes from two places:

1. Reduced expenses — Rapid operates with lower overhead, a leaner corporate structure, and shared services that reduce Austin's branch-level cost burden.

2. Ancillary income — Rapid offers profit sharing, equity participation, or a combination that does not exist at Lower. This is incremental income on top of branch net profit.

8 Honest Tradeoffs

No move is without risk. Here is what Austin should weigh.

Where Risk Exists

  • Brand recognition: Lower is a larger, more recognized name. Rapid does not have the same national consumer awareness. Austin would rely more heavily on his personal brand and referral network, which is already strong — but it is a real factor.
  • Transition friction: Any move creates 30-60 days of pipeline disruption. Loans in process, realtor relationships, referral partner communication. This is temporary but real.
  • Smaller company risk: Rapid is a smaller operation. That is the source of the cost advantage, but it also means less institutional depth. If something goes wrong at the corporate level, there are fewer buffers.
  • Benefits gap: If Lower provides health insurance, 401(k) match, or other employment benefits, those have real value ($15K-30K/yr). Austin should calculate what he currently receives and factor it against the financial advantage.
  • Expense assumptions: The expense estimates in this analysis are based on industry norms. If Austin's actual expenses at Lower are materially lower than estimated, the savings gap narrows. Austin should run this with his real numbers.

Where Rapid Wins

  • Lower operating costs: Smaller company, less overhead, shared services model. The expense savings are structural, not promotional.
  • Ancillary income: Profit share and/or equity that simply do not exist at Lower. This is additive to the branch net profit.
  • Autonomy and control: Austin controls his branch operations, marketing, and team decisions without corporate layers.
  • Ownership trajectory: Equity in a growing company is wealth creation. No amount of commission splits at a large IMB replicates this.
  • Direct partnership: Working directly with owners vs. being one branch among many in a large organization.
Bottom line: The financial advantage is real but not a 3x multiple — it is a meaningful improvement in take-home plus a category of income (ancillary) that does not exist in the current structure. The qualitative benefits — autonomy, ownership, leadership access — are what make this a career-level decision, not just a comp negotiation.
