

# VA LOAN GUIDELINES — Quick Reference

## 1. Eligibility — Who Qualifies, COE Requirements

**Service requirements to establish eligibility:**

- **Wartime veterans:** 90 days active duty (Gulf War era: 8/2/1990–present still applies to most current borrowers)
- **Peacetime veterans:** 181 continuous days active duty
- **National Guard / Reserves:** 6 years in the Selected Reserve, OR 90 days of active federal service under Title 10 orders
- **Surviving spouses:** Un-remarried spouse of a veteran who died in service or from a service-connected disability; also spouses who remarried after age 57 and after 12/16/2003

**Certificate of Eligibility (COE):**
- Obtained via VA's eBenefits portal, by mail (VA Form 26-1880), or through the lender's Web LGY/ACE system (fastest — usually instant)
- The COE shows the veteran's entitlement amount and any prior VA loan usage
- **Key difference from FHA/conventional:** There is no equivalent document in conventional or FHA lending. The COE is a prerequisite unique to VA.

---

## 2. No Down Payment / No PMI Advantage

- **0% down payment** on purchase loans up to the conforming loan limit ($766,550 in most counties for 2025; higher in high-cost areas) — as long as the veteran has full entitlement available.
- Veterans with reduced (partially used) entitlement can still get 0% down, but the no-down-payment amount is capped by their remaining entitlement.
- **No monthly mortgage insurance (MI/PMI) — ever.** This is the single biggest cost advantage over FHA (which charges both UFMIP and annual MIP for the life of the loan on most terms) and conventional (which requires PMI when LTV exceeds 80%).
- On a $400,000 loan, eliminating PMI saves roughly $150–$250/month compared to FHA or conventional with less than 20% down.

---

## 3. Funding Fee Structure and Exemptions

The VA Funding Fee replaces monthly MI as a one-time charge. It can be financed into the loan.

**Purchase & Construction Loans (effective as of current VA Circular):**

| Down Payment | First Use | Subsequent Use |
|---|---|---|
| 0% down | 2.15% | 3.30% |
| 5%–9.99% down | 1.50% | 1.50% |
| 10%+ down | 1.25% | 1.25% |

**Cash-Out Refinance:** 2.15% (first use), 3.30% (subsequent use)

**IRRRL (Streamline Refi):** 0.50% (all borrowers)

**Exemptions — No Funding Fee Charged To:**
- Veterans receiving VA disability compensation (any rating, even 0% with compensation)
- Veterans rated eligible for compensation but receiving retirement pay instead
- Surviving spouses (those eligible for DIC or listed MIA/POW)
- Active duty Purple Heart recipients
- **Key difference:** FHA charges UFMIP to everyone regardless of disability status. The VA exemption is a significant benefit for disabled veterans.

---

## 4. Credit Requirements

- **VA itself sets NO minimum credit score.** This is a critical difference from FHA (which functionally requires 500+ and practically 580+ for 3.5% down) and conventional (typically 620+).
- **Lender overlays** are the real gatekeepers. Most mid-size lenders set internal minimums of **620 FICO**, with some going to 580 for manual underwrite scenarios.
- **AUS:** VA loans run through VA's own automated system or through GUS (for USDA — not applicable here) or standard DU/LPA with VA-specific settings. If the AUS returns an "Accept/Eligible" or "Approve/Eligible," most lenders will go to 620. Manual underwrite cases typically require compensating factors.
- **Bankruptcy/foreclosure waiting periods:**
  - Chapter 7 BK: **2 years** from discharge (vs. 4 years conventional, 2 years FHA)
  - Chapter 13 BK: **1 year** of on-time payments with court approval (vs. 2 years FHA from discharge, 4 years conventional)
  - Foreclosure: **2 years** (vs. 7 years conventional, 3 years FHA)
  - These shorter waiting periods are a major VA advantage.

---

## 5. DTI Limits

- **AUS Approve/Eligible:** No hard DTI cap. The AUS will assess risk holistically. In practice, DTI ratios of **55–60%+** can receive automated approvals if strong compensating factors exist (significant residual income, excellent credit, substantial assets).
- **Manual underwrite:** VA Circular and Lender Handbook (Chapter 4) guide to a **41% DTI** benchmark, but this can be exceeded with documented compensating factors:
  - Residual income exceeding the guideline by 20%+
  - Minimal consumer debt
  - Conservative use of credit
  - Military benefits (tax-free BAH/BAS) that effectively reduce tax burden
- **Key difference from conventional/FHA:** VA is the most flexible government program on DTI. Conventional caps at 50% DTI (DU) in most cases; FHA typically caps at 56.99% with AUS approval but is often tighter in practice.

---

## 6. Residual Income Requirements (Unique to VA)

**This is the single most distinctive VA underwriting concept.** No other loan program uses it.

Residual income = gross monthly income minus federal/state taxes, Social Security, retirement, job-related expenses, shelter expense (PITI + HOA + utilities maintenance estimate), and all recurring debts. What's left must meet or exceed the VA's table minimums.

**VA Residual Income Minimums by Region and Family Size (Monthly):**

| Family Size | Northeast | Midwest | South | West |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $450 | $441 | $441 | $491 |
| 2 | $755 | $738 | $738 | $823 |
| 3 | $909 | $889 | $889 | $990 |
| 4 | $1,025 | $1,003 | $1,003 | $1,117 |
| 5 | $1,062 | $1,039 | $1,039 | $1,158 |
| Each add'l | +$80 | +$80 | +$80 | +$80 |

*For loan amounts over $80,000 (nearly all loans today), the above table applies. Loans at or below $79,999 use a lower table.*

- On **manual underwrite**, residual income is a hard requirement and cannot be waived.
- On **AUS-approved** loans, residual income is still calculated but the automated finding can compensate for a minor shortfall.
- **Compensating factor:** If residual income exceeds the guideline by **20% or more**, it can offset a high DTI on manual underwrites.

---

## 7. Property Requirements — VA Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs)

VA MPRs are similar in spirit to FHA's Minimum Property Standards but have some distinctions:

- **Must be move-in ready.** No major deferred maintenance. Roof must be functional, mechanical systems operational, no health/safety hazards.
- **Adequate heating** in all habitable rooms (a common trip-up in rural properties).
- **Safe potable water and sanitary sewage** disposal.
- **No wood-destroying insect (termite) damage** — VA requires a termite inspection in most states (state-specific; not required in Alaska, for example). The veteran is **not permitted to pay** for the termite inspection in most cases — this is typically a seller or agent cost. This is unique to VA.
- **Crawl space requirements:** Minimum 18 inches clearance from ground to bottom of joists; must be accessible; vapor barrier required over exposed earth.
- **Lead-based paint:** Same federal requirements as FHA for pre-1978 homes.
- **Appraisal is performed by a VA-assigned fee panel appraiser** through the VA portal. The lender cannot select the appraiser. Appraisals carry a Tidewater process: if the appraiser anticipates the value will come in below contract price, they must invoke Tidewater, which gives the lender/agent 2 business days to provide additional comparable sales.
- **Appraisal validity:** 6 months (same as FHA), and the appraisal stays with the property (not the borrower), meaning a second VA buyer can use the same appraisal if it's still valid.

---

## 8. Entitlement — Basic and Bonus Entitlement

This is the most commonly misunderstood VA concept. Here is a simplified breakdown:

**Basic Entitlement:** $36,000
- This is the baseline set by law. By itself, it would only support a loan up to $144,000 (since VA guarantees 25% of the loan: $36,000 x 4 = $144,000).

**Bonus (Tier 2) Entitlement:** Up to 25% of the conforming loan limit minus the basic entitlement
- In a standard county (2025 limit $766,550): 25% = $191,637.50 total guarantee capacity.
- Minus the $36,000 basic = $155,637.50 in bonus entitlement.
- Combined: $191,637.50 guaranty supports a $766,550 loan at 0% down.

**Partial Entitlement / Second-Tier Use:**
- A veteran can have more than one VA loan at a time if they have remaining entitlement.
- Example: Veteran used $50,000 of entitlement on a prior home they still own. Remaining entitlement = $191,637.50 – $50,000 = $141,637.50, supporting a second VA loan up to approximately $566,550 at 0% down.
- Entitlement is **restored** when the prior VA loan is paid off AND the property is sold (or via a one-time restoration if the prior loan is paid off but the veteran retains the property).

**Loans above the conforming limit:**
- With full entitlement, veterans can borrow above the county loan limit with 0% down (this changed with the Blue Water Navy Act effective 1/1/2020).
- With partial entitlement, a down payment of 25% of the difference between the purchase price and the remaining entitlement-supported amount is required.

---

## 9. VA IRRRL (Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan) — Streamline Refi

**Key rules that differentiate the IRRRL:**

- **Must be VA-to-VA.** The existing loan being refinanced must be a VA loan.
- **No appraisal required** (though a lender may order one for internal risk purposes).
- **No income verification or DTI calculation required** by VA. The lender must only confirm the veteran is making current payments.
- **Net tangible benefit test:** The new loan must provide a net tangible benefit:
  - Rate must decrease by at least 0.50% (50 bps) if going from fixed to fixed.
  - If going from ARM to fixed, a rate increase is permitted as long as the borrower gains rate stability.
  - If the veteran is adding energy-efficient improvements (up to $6,000), the rate can increase slightly.
- **Funding fee:** 0.50% (all borrowers; exempt veterans pay $0).
- **Maximum loan amount:** Limited to the existing VA loan balance + allowable closing costs + up to 2 discount points + funding fee. **No cash out** is permitted on an IRRRL.
- **Occupancy:** The veteran must certify they **previously occupied** the home. They do not need to currently live there (unlike a VA purchase, which requires owner-occupancy intent).
- **Seasoning:** At least **210 days** must have passed since the first payment of the loan being refinanced, AND at least **6 monthly payments** must have been made.
- **Key difference from FHA Streamline:** Very similar in concept, but FHA Streamline requires the borrower to have made at least 6 payments and 210 days from closing (not first payment). VA's net tangible benefit rules are somewhat stricter than FHA's.

# USDA LOAN GUIDELINES — Quick Reference

## 1. Eligibility — Income Limits and Geographic Requirements

**This is what makes USDA unique among all mortgage programs.** Both the borrower's income and the property location must qualify.

**Income Limits:**
- Household income cannot exceed **115% of the area median income (AMI)** for the county where the property is located.
- **Critical:** USDA counts **all adult household members' income**, not just the borrowers on the loan. If a non-borrowing spouse or adult child living in the home earns income, it counts toward the household income cap even though it is not used to qualify for the loan.
- Income limits vary by county and family size. Example ranges (2024-2025):
  - 1-4 person household in a typical Midwest rural county: approximately $110,650
  - 5-8 person household: approximately $146,050
  - High-cost areas can be higher
- Look up specific limits at: **https://eligibility.sc.egov.usda.gov/eligibility/incomeEligibilityAction.do**
- **Deductions allowed:** Childcare costs for children under 12, certain disability-related deductions, elderly household deductions ($480/year), and dependents ($480 per dependent) can be subtracted from household income to get under the cap.

**Geographic Requirements:**
- Property must be in a **USDA-designated rural area**. Defined as areas with populations generally under 35,000 (though some grandfathered areas with populations up to 35,000 remain eligible).
- Approximately **97% of the U.S. land mass** qualifies, covering areas where roughly 30% of the population lives.
- Many suburban fringe areas qualify — this surprises borrowers. Properties just outside metro boundaries often are eligible.
- Look up property eligibility at: **https://eligibility.sc.egov.usda.gov/eligibility/welcomeAction.do**
- USDA periodically re-maps eligible areas. Areas can lose eligibility as populations grow (though Congress has repeatedly extended grandfathered areas).

---

## 2. No Down Payment Advantage

- **100% financing (0% down)** — same as VA in this regard.
- Unlike VA, there is no entitlement system. If the borrower and property qualify, 0% down is available regardless of prior use.
- **Key difference from FHA:** FHA requires 3.5% minimum down. Conventional requires 3-5% minimum. USDA and VA are the only 0%-down programs.
- The guarantee/annual fees (below) can be financed into the loan, meaning the borrower can truly come to closing with $0 down — though they still need funds for earnest money and any prepaid/escrow items not covered by seller concessions.
- **Seller concessions:** Up to **6% of the purchase price** can be contributed by the seller toward the buyer's closing costs (same as FHA; conventional allows 3% at >90% LTV).

---

## 3. Guarantee Fee and Annual Fee

USDA charges two fees that function like FHA's UFMIP and annual MIP:

**Upfront Guarantee Fee:**
- **1.00%** of the loan amount (as of current rates; this was reduced from 2.00% in October 2016)
- Can be financed into the loan amount
- Example: $200,000 purchase price, 0% down = $200,000 base loan + $2,000 guarantee fee = $202,000 total loan

**Annual Fee (equivalent to monthly MI):**
- **0.35%** of the outstanding loan balance per year, paid monthly
- Example: On a $200,000 balance, annual fee = $700/year = $58.33/month
- This is significantly cheaper than FHA annual MIP (0.55% for most FHA loans with >95% LTV)
- **The annual fee does NOT drop off** based on LTV like conventional PMI. It remains for the life of the loan — similar to FHA's life-of-loan MIP on loans with <10% down.
- **Key cost comparison:**

| Fee Type | USDA | FHA | Conventional (5% down) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Upfront | 1.00% | 1.75% | None |
| Monthly rate | 0.35%/yr | 0.55%/yr | ~0.40-0.80%/yr (varies by credit/LTV) |
| Duration | Life of loan | Life of loan (most cases) | Until 78-80% LTV |

---

## 4. Credit Requirements

- **GUS (Guaranteed Underwriting System)** is USDA's automated underwriting system. It is the preferred path.
- **GUS Accept:** Minimum **640 FICO** required for automated underwriting. Most lenders will not go below 640 for USDA.
- **Manual underwrite:** USDA allows manual underwriting for borrowers below 640 FICO, but most lenders have overlays that require 640+ regardless. For lenders that do allow manual UW, a minimum of **620-640** is typical, with strict compensating factor requirements.
- **No GUS for scores below 620** — must be manual underwrite with significant compensating factors.
- **Bankruptcy/foreclosure waiting periods:**
  - Chapter 7 BK: **3 years** from discharge (vs. 2 years VA, 2 years FHA, 4 years conventional)
  - Chapter 13 BK: **1 year** of on-time payout with court approval (similar to VA and FHA)
  - Foreclosure: **3 years** from transfer date (vs. 2 years VA, 3 years FHA, 7 years conventional)
- **Housing history:** USDA requires a 12-month clean housing payment history for GUS Accept files. For manual UW, this is critical and a single 30-day late mortgage/rent payment in the prior 12 months is typically disqualifying.

---

## 5. DTI Limits

- **GUS Accept:** The system will evaluate DTI holistically, but the general guideline is:
  - **Front-end (housing) DTI:** 29% (this is a guideline, not a hard cap, when GUS approves)
  - **Back-end (total) DTI:** 41% (same caveat — GUS can approve higher)
  - In practice, GUS will approve DTIs up to approximately **44-46%** with strong compensating factors (excellent credit, low payment shock, significant reserves).
- **Manual underwrite:** Hard cap of **29% front / 41% back.** These are firm and cannot be exceeded, unlike VA manual UW where compensating factors can push past 41%.
- **Key difference from VA/FHA/conventional:** USDA is the most restrictive on DTI. VA has no hard cap on AUS approvals. FHA allows up to 56.99% back-end on AUS. Conventional (DU) allows up to 50%. USDA's 41% manual cap and ~44-46% GUS ceiling make it the tightest program for debt-heavy borrowers.

---

## 6. Property Eligibility Lookup

- **Eligibility map tool:** https://eligibility.sc.egov.usda.gov/eligibility/welcomeAction.do
- Enter the property address; the system returns eligible or ineligible.
- **Property must be:**
  - A single-family residence (1 unit only — no 2-4 units, unlike FHA/VA/conventional)
  - The borrower's **primary residence** (no investment properties, no second homes)
  - Modest in size, design, and cost for the area (no luxury features; in-ground pools are generally acceptable if typical for the area, but must not contribute excessive value)
  - Site condos and PUDs are eligible; standard condos may be eligible with project approval
  - **No income-producing features** — properties with significant agricultural operations, outbuildings used for commercial purposes, or acreage valued above 30% of total appraised value may be ineligible
  - **Manufactured homes** are eligible if permanently affixed to a foundation meeting HUD standards, on a permanent site, and classified as real property
- **Appraisal:** Performed by a standard FHA/VA-approved appraiser. USDA uses the same appraisal forms and standards as FHA (including MPR-style property condition requirements). The appraisal must confirm the property is in a USDA-eligible area.

---

## 7. Income Calculation Differences

This is where USDA diverges most significantly from every other loan program. Loan officers must understand **two separate income analyses:**

**A. Qualifying Income (for DTI calculation):**
- Standard income analysis — count only the borrowers' stable, verifiable income.
- Same approach as FHA/VA/conventional: W-2, paystubs, tax returns, VOE, etc.
- Self-employment income: 2-year history, averaged or declining analysis (same as other programs).

**B. Household Income (for the 115% AMI eligibility cap):**
- Count **ALL income from ALL adult (18+) household members**, whether or not they are on the loan.
- Includes:
  - Non-borrowing spouse's income
  - Adult children living in the home (even if they are not on the loan or the title)
  - Any other adult household member's income
  - Part-time, seasonal, Social Security, disability, pension, alimony received, child support received
- **This catches many loan officers off guard.** A borrower may qualify on DTI with their own income, but the combined household income may push them over the AMI cap, making them ineligible.
- **Allowable deductions from household income:**
  - $480 per minor dependent (under 18) or full-time student dependent (18-23)
  - $480 for any elderly household member (62+)
  - Childcare expenses for children under 12 (actual documented costs)
  - Medical expenses for elderly family members exceeding 3% of gross income
  - Disability assistance expenses

**Example scenario illustrating the difference:**
- Borrower earns $75,000/year. Non-borrowing spouse earns $35,000/year. Two minor children.
- Qualifying income (DTI): $75,000 (borrower only, if spouse is not on the loan)
- Household income (eligibility): $75,000 + $35,000 = $110,000, minus $960 (2 dependents x $480) = $109,040 adjusted household income. If the county AMI cap for a 4-person household is $110,650, they are under the limit and eligible. This is a tight margin and underscores why the deductions matter.

**Other income nuances unique to USDA:**
- **Assets as imputed income:** If a borrower has liquid assets exceeding $50,000 (after subtracting funds needed for closing), USDA may impute income from those assets at a rate determined by the current passbook savings rate. This imputed amount is added to household income for the eligibility cap calculation. No other program does this.
- **Temporary leave / seasonal income:** Must document the likelihood of continuation. USDA follows standard Handbook guidelines but the household income overlay makes seasonal or variable income particularly tricky.

## Key Comparison Summary: What Makes VA and USDA Different

| Feature | VA | USDA | FHA | Conventional |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Down payment | 0% | 0% | 3.5% | 3-5% |
| Monthly MI/fee | None | 0.35%/yr | 0.55%/yr | Varies, drops at 80% LTV |
| Upfront fee | 2.15% (first use, 0% down) | 1.00% | 1.75% | None |
| Min credit (program) | None (lender overlays apply) | 640 (GUS) | 580 (3.5% down) | 620 typical |
| DTI flexibility | Most flexible | Most restrictive | Moderate | Moderate |
| Geographic restriction | None | Rural areas only | None | None |
| Income limits | None | 115% AMI | None | None |
| Occupancy | Owner-occupied | Owner-occupied | Owner-occupied | Owner/2nd/Investment |
| Property types | 1-4 units | 1 unit only | 1-4 units | 1-4 units |
| Unique feature | Residual income test, entitlement system | Household income test, area eligibility | Life-of-loan MIP | PMI cancellation |

---

This reference covers the core differentiators. The critical takeaways for your loan officers:

1. **VA** is the most borrower-friendly program overall — no MI, no down payment, most flexible DTI, shortest BK/foreclosure waiting periods. The funding fee is the trade-off, but exempt veterans avoid even that. The residual income requirement is the one area where VA adds a unique underwriting hurdle not found elsewhere.

2. **USDA** is the most cost-effective option for eligible borrowers (lowest combined upfront + monthly fees), but it is the most restrictive on who and where: income caps, geographic limits, 1-unit only, and the tightest DTI ceilings among government programs. The household income calculation is the #1 disqualifier that catches borrowers and loan officers by surprise.
